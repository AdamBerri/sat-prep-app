Here's something most prep courses won't tell you: **official College Board questions are a limited resource, and you shouldn't waste them on everyday practice.**

Let us explain why—and show you exactly when to use them for maximum impact.

## The Official Question Shortage

College Board has released a finite number of official practice tests. Depending on when you're reading this, there might be 6-10 full official practice tests available. That's it. Once you've seen a question, you can never truly "unsee" it.

Every official question you use during casual practice is one less question that feels fresh during your final preparation. And that freshness matters more than you think.

## The Two Times to Use Official Questions

We recommend using official College Board materials at exactly two points in your prep journey:

### 1. Your Baseline Diagnostic

Before you start serious prep, take ONE official practice test under realistic conditions. This tells you:

- Your starting score
- Which sections need the most work
- How you handle test timing and stamina
- What "real" SAT questions feel like

<Tip>
Take your diagnostic on a Saturday morning, timed, in a quiet room. Simulate the real experience as closely as possible. This is your benchmark—everything you do from here will be measured against it.
</Tip>

After this diagnostic, put the official tests away. Seriously. Lock them up mentally. You won't need them for months.

### 2. The Final 2-3 Weeks

This is when official tests become invaluable. In the weeks leading up to your actual SAT:

- Take one official test per week
- Simulate real testing conditions exactly
- Review your mistakes carefully
- Track your progress against your baseline

These final practice tests serve a different purpose than your daily practice. They're not for learning—they're for **calibration**. You're getting your brain used to the exact experience of the real test.

<Warning title="Don't Peek Ahead">
Never "preview" official questions. Don't flip through tests to "see what's there." Every question you casually glance at is a question that won't feel new when you need it to.
</Warning>

## The Psychology of Fresh Questions

Here's why saving official tests matters psychologically:

When you take an official test and it feels new, your brain processes it the way it will process the real test. You experience authentic problem-solving, genuine time pressure, and real decision-making.

When you take an official test and recognize questions from previous practice, something different happens. Your brain partially retrieves memories instead of fully solving problems. Your timing is off because you remember some answers. The experience doesn't match what the real test will feel like.

<Highlight>
Your final practice tests should feel like dress rehearsals, not review sessions. Save that fresh experience for when it counts.
</Highlight>

## A Strategic Timeline

Here's a sample timeline for a student with 3 months to prep:

**Month 1:**
- Take ONE official test for your baseline
- Daily practice with the1600club questions
- Focus on understanding concepts and building habits

**Month 2:**
- Continue daily practice
- Targeted work on weak areas identified in your baseline
- NO official tests—just keep building skills

**Month 3, Weeks 1-2:**
- Continue daily practice
- Start increasing session length to build stamina
- Still no official tests

**Month 3, Weeks 3-4:**
- Take official test #2, review thoroughly
- Take official test #3 the following weekend
- Light practice between tests, focusing on weak spots
- Take official test #4 a few days before the real thing (optional)

## Practice vs. Simulation

It helps to think about two different modes of preparation:

**Practice Mode** (daily, 80% of your prep time):
- Learning new concepts
- Building speed and accuracy
- Making mistakes and learning from them
- Using third-party questions liberally

**Simulation Mode** (final weeks, 20% of your prep time):
- Full-length, timed tests
- Exact test-day conditions
- Using official questions
- Calibrating expectations and timing

Both modes are essential. But mixing them up—using official questions for daily practice, or doing casual untimed work in your final weeks—wastes resources and reduces effectiveness.
